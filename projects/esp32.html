Custom circuit board design and firmware development for MIT's Electronics for Mechanical Systems II.

Project 1: The Screaming Gingerbread Man

My first project for the course was a Gingerbread Man PCB designed to "scream" if one of its limbs was broken off. The system utilized an Arduino Nano to detect voltage drops across resistors placed in parallel with breakaway wire traces on the board's limbs.

A DF Player Mini handles audio playback from a microSD card. When the initial PCB revision failed to trigger sound, I isolated the hardware logic by recreating the circuit on a breadboard. This systematic debugging revealed a few minor trace issues, as well as a critical UART schematic error: I had routed the Nano's RX and TX pins directly to the DF Player's RX and TX, rather than correctly crossing them over.

Project 2: Wireless Guitar Hero Controller

For my final project, I engineered a custom, wireless Guitar Hero controller from scratch. The system is built around the ESP32-S3 C1 devkit. This specific microcontroller was selected because of its native USB Human Interface Device (HID) capabilities, dedicated D+ and D- pins for USB data, and onboard Bluetooth for untethered gameplay.

To make the controller fully wireless, I designed a power management system utilizing a 3.7V 18650 battery with built-in charge protection, leveraging the main board's charging circuit. The controller switches seamlessly between USB and battery power, using a Low-Dropout (LDO) regulator to supply 5V, with dedicated LEDs indicating the charging state.

Navigating Hardware Constraints

Developing custom hardware requires navigating unexpected constraints and layout errors. On the first PCB revision, the board failed to boot because I had inadvertently tied an active-high enable pin directly to ground. Bending the pin and connecting to high was not enough to fix the error.

Additionally, during firmware development, I had to remap my fret inputs after identifying internal constraints on pins like Pin 37. I ultimately routed the fret buttons to pins 45, 48, 47, 21, and 14, which provided reliable, uninterrupted HID communication for gameplay. By implementing hardware workarounds for these layout bugs, I was able to successfully demonstrate a fully functional, playable guitar controller for the final presentation.

Hardware Architecture: ESP32 Controller

Tap the markers to explore the core subsystems.

ESP32-S3 C1

Selected for its native USB HID support. Fret and button inputs are routed to digital pins on the right hemisphere of the board. D+ and D- USB data traces were kept as short as possible to minimize signal degradation and noise. Power is delivered at 3.3V, utilizing bypass capacitors to smooth current fluctuations.

Ground Plane

The back of the PCB acts as a continuous ground plane to simplify routing and improve signal integrity. Strict copper keep-out zones were implemented around the mounting holes to prevent metal fasteners from shorting the circuit. Thickened power traces ensure safe current delivery to the LDO.

V2 Roadmap

While the initial board required hardware workarounds to become playable, diagnosing these layout bugs provided a clear roadmap for the next iteration. For V2, I plan to implement the following changes:

Guitar

  • Correct grounded active-high enable pin.
  • Optimize the fret input trace routing, permanently avoiding internally constrained GPIOs like Pin 37.
  • Connect type-C 5V pin to ESP32 to switch from wired to wireless mode.
  • Add protection circuit to main PCB.
  • Use correct Low-profile switch footprint.
  • Integrate the ESP32 directly into guitar PCB.

Gingerbread

  • Connect RX to TX wires and vice-versa.
  • Add 100 Ohm resistor between DF RX and Nano TX for signal integrity.
  • Use correct Molex connector for battery.
  • Replace Nano for ATmega microcontroller.
