FF11K Marine Lander CFD

Simulating extreme deep-sea hydrodynamics and predicting descent trajectories for an 11km-rated modular marine lander during the MISTI Japan program.

View Slide Deck (PDF)

Overview: MISTI Japan Program

Over the course of a two-month placement with the MISTI Japan program, I was tasked with modeling the descent trajectory of the FF11K lander. Designed to drop 11 kilometers to study the Hadal zone, the modular lander faces severe ocean currents in the first 1.5 km of its descent, causing significant operational drift. To ensure accurate deployment and recovery, the team required a precise predictive model of the vehicle's hydrodynamic behavior.

Computational Fluid Dynamics Pipeline

To tackle this, I developed a simulation pipeline to calculate the extreme forces and torques acting on the lander using OpenFOAM, an open-source CFD solver. I utilized simpleFoam to compute the steady-state forces acting on the geometry, subsequently fitting the output data to a quadratic drag model.

To account for the vehicle's rotational dynamics during free-fall, I ran an Arbitrary Mesh Interface (AMI) simulation to extract the precise torque values generated by the lander's geometry.

Automation and Solver Limitations

Because these simulations were highly computationally expensive, I wrote custom Bash scripts to fully automate the OpenFOAM execution pipeline, allowing for continuous, overnight data collection without manual intervention.

I also attempted to use pimpleFoam to solve for the lander's added mass. However, navigating the limitations of the solver proved challenging; the pressure values consistently diverged, which compromised the data quality and resulted in exceedingly long runtimes.

Validation and Retrospective

When validating the final trajectory model against real-world telemetry from the physical lander, the prediction drifted significantly—particularly after the 1.5 km mark—and exhibited excessive rotation that did not match the physical behavior.

This project served as a rigorous introduction to the complexities and extreme sensitivities of numerical fluid dynamics. Navigating the steep learning curve of raw OpenFOAM solvers highlighted the rapid evolution of modern computational engineering workflows. Reflecting on the manual mesh tuning and solver instabilities, it is clear that integrating modern AI-assisted physics models would likely trivialize much of the computational friction I encountered, drastically accelerating the iteration cycle for deep-sea hydrodynamic modeling.
